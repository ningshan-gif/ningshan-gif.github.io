This project explores a question I find both technical and human: when language models are asked to respond as many different kinds of people, do they actually preserve meaningful differences, or do they collapse distinct personas into a much smaller set of repeated behavioral patterns?

I am intentionally keeping this page high-level, because the project is still better represented as an active research direction than as a fully public technical release. At a broad level, the work studies how model outputs cluster, how much variation survives across prompts and identities, and whether current systems flatten complex human characteristics into overly simple response styles.

What interests me most here is not just model performance, but representation. If AI systems are going to stand in for people, simulate users, or personalize interfaces, then it matters whether they can sustain nuance rather than defaulting to a few stereotyped modes. This project sits at the intersection of language models, behavioral modeling, and the question of how much individuality survives once a person becomes data.
